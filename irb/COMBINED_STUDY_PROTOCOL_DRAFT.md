## 1. BACKGROUND AND RATIONALE

### 1.1 Scientific Background

#### 1.1.1 EEG as a Waking Emotion Biomarker

Electroencephalography (EEG) measures electrical brain activity non-invasively
via scalp electrodes. The following biomarkers are most validated for emotion:

- **Frontal Alpha Asymmetry (FAA):** ln(right_alpha) − ln(left_alpha). Positive
  FAA = approach motivation / positive affect. Negative FAA = withdrawal / negative
  affect. 30+ years of replication (Davidson, 1992; Coan & Allen, 2004).
- **High-beta power (20–30 Hz):** Correlates with stress and anxiety
  (Giannakakis et al., 2019).
- **Frontal Midline Theta (FMT, 4–8 Hz):** Correlates with self-regulatory
  processing and emotion regulation (Klimesch, 1999).

Consumer-grade EEG headsets — particularly the Muse 2 (InteraXon Inc.) — have
made wearable EEG feasible outside laboratory settings. Validation studies confirm
the Muse 2 can reliably measure FAA and band power features for emotion-related
research in naturalistic conditions, with important limitations acknowledged
(Cannard et al., 2021; Krigolson et al., 2017).

#### 1.1.2 The Continuity Hypothesis of Dreaming

The **Continuity Hypothesis** (Schredl, 2003; Hartmann, 1995) is among the most
replicated and widely accepted theories in dream science:

> Dream content is a continuation of waking emotional concerns. The emotional
> themes, intensities, and valences that dominate waking life are reflected in
> the content and affect of that night's dreams.

Supporting evidence includes:
- Negative waking affect predicts increased nightmare frequency and dream
  negativity (Schredl & Reinhard, 2011; Levin & Nielsen, 2007)
- Acute stressors (exam stress, bereavement, combat exposure) produce measurable
  shifts in dream content within 24–72 hours (De Koninck, 2012)
- REM sleep is the primary stage for emotional memory processing and consolidation
  (Walker & Stickgold, 2006; Hobson et al., 2000)
- Disruption of REM sleep specifically impairs emotional memory processing and
  next-day affective regulation (Walker, 2009)

**The critical gap:** All existing tests of the continuity hypothesis have used
either self-report of waking emotion (no objective measure) or laboratory
polysomnography (not naturalistic). No study has tested whether objectively
measured daytime EEG emotion biomarkers — collected in participants' real-world
lives — predict that night's dream emotional content in home sleep. This is the
primary contribution of the present study.

#### 1.1.3 Eating Behavior and the Emotion-Dream Pipeline

Emotional eating — consuming food in response to emotional states rather than
physical hunger — is associated with elevated nighttime cortisol, disrupted sleep
architecture, and increased REM dream negativity (Yau & Potenza, 2013; Sinha &
Jastreboff, 2013). The emotional state that drives emotional eating may thus
function as a moderating variable in the daytime emotion → dream content pathway:

- A person who experiences stress but eats in response to it (stress eating) may
  show a different emotion-dream relationship than a person who experiences
  equivalent stress but does not stress-eat.
- Mindful eating, associated with lower post-meal cortisol and better emotional
  regulation, may buffer the transmission of daytime stress into nighttime dreams.

This moderating role of eating behavior in the emotion-dream pathway has not been
examined in any published study.

#### 1.1.4 Nightmare Prediction and Clinical Relevance

Nightmare disorder affects approximately 4% of adults and is comorbid with PTSD,
depression, anxiety, and sleep disorders (American Academy of Sleep Medicine, 2014).
Current treatments are reactive — applied after nightmares begin. A system capable
of predicting nightmare-prone nights from daytime physiological signals would
enable proactive intervention (e.g., pre-sleep relaxation, imagery rehearsal
therapy initiation).

High-beta EEG power during waking hours — a validated stress/anxiety biomarker —
is a candidate predictor of nightmare occurrence that night. If this relationship
holds in naturalistic data, consumer-grade EEG could become the first wearable
tool for prospective nightmare risk detection.

### 1.2 The Emotional Transfer Chain: Broader Research Program Context

This study is the first empirical phase of a multi-phase program investigating the
**Emotional Transfer Chain** — the hypothesis that emotional states propagate through
the food chain (grower → cook → server → eater) and, by extension, through the
day-night cycle (waking emotion → eating behavior → sleep architecture → dream
content). Section 1.2 of the Phase 1 pilot protocol (01_STUDY_PROTOCOL.md v1.1)
provides full detail on the cross-cultural and scientific evidence for this broader
program. The present study constitutes **Phase 1B** of that program, directly
testing one link of the chain: the waking emotion → eating → dream pathway.

### 1.3 Specific Aims

**Aim 1 — Continuity Hypothesis (Primary):**
Test whether daytime EEG-derived emotion biomarkers (FAA, high-beta, FMT)
measured in naturalistic conditions predict that night's self-reported dream
emotional valence. We hypothesize that negative daytime FAA and elevated daytime
high-beta will predict more negative dream affect, consistent with the continuity
hypothesis (H1).

**Aim 2 — Eating Behavior as Moderator:**
Examine whether eating motivation type (emotional vs. physical hunger vs. habit)
moderates the daytime emotion → dream content relationship. We hypothesize that
emotional eating days will show a stronger positive correlation between daytime
stress EEG and nighttime dream negativity than physical hunger days, reflecting
disrupted emotional regulation via food (H2).

**Aim 3 — Nightmare Prediction:**
Assess whether daytime high-beta EEG power, negative FAA, and self-reported
stress level prospectively predict nightmare occurrence that night. We hypothesize
that days with high-beta EEG in the top quartile and negative FAA will be
associated with significantly elevated nightmare probability (H3).

**Aim 4 — Longitudinal EEG Emotion Reliability:**
Characterize the within-person stability of consumer-grade EEG emotion biomarkers
over 30 days, establishing the first longitudinal reliability benchmark for Muse 2
emotion monitoring in naturalistic conditions.

### 1.4 Hypotheses

**(H1)** Days with negative daytime FAA (< 0) and/or elevated high-beta power
(top tertile) will be followed by dream affect ratings ≥ 1 point lower on a 9-point
SAM valence scale than days with positive FAA and low high-beta.

**(H2)** The daytime-emotion → dream-valence correlation will be significantly
stronger on emotional eating days (unstandardized β difference ≥ 0.20) than on
physical hunger eating days, controlling for overall stress level.

**(H3)** High-beta EEG (top quartile) + negative FAA will predict nightmare
occurrence with AUC ≥ 0.70 in a mixed-effects logistic regression.

**(H4)** Within-person test-retest reliability (ICC) of daily FAA across the
30-day period will be ≥ 0.50, consistent with published FAA reliability benchmarks.

## 2. STUDY DESIGN

**Design:** Longitudinal, within-person, observational, repeated-measures.
**Duration:** 30 consecutive days per participant.
**Setting:** Participants' natural environment (home). All data collected via
the NeuralDreamWorkshop app. No laboratory visits required after onboarding.
**Sessions per participant:**
- 1 onboarding session (remote or in-person, ~60 min)
- 30 daily data collection cycles (self-administered via app)
- 1 offboarding session (remote, ~20 min)

### 2.1 Daily Data Collection Cycle

Each day consists of three brief data collection moments. Total daily burden:
approximately 25–35 minutes, spread across the day.

---

**MORNING (5–10 min) — within 30 minutes of waking**

| Step | Activity | Duration |
|------|----------|----------|
| M1 | Dream journal: free-text entry of any remembered dream content | 3–5 min |
| M2 | Dream affect rating: SAM valence + arousal for the dream (or "no recall") | 1 min |
| M3 | Nightmare flag: yes / no / unsure | 30 sec |
| M4 | Sleep quality rating: 1–9 scale | 30 sec |
| M5 | Sleep duration: estimated hours slept | 30 sec |

> **Why immediately on waking:** Dream recall decays within 5–10 minutes of
> waking. The morning data collection must be completed before checking phone
> notifications, getting out of bed, or speaking to others. Participants are
> instructed to keep the app open on their nightstand screen.

---

**DAYTIME (15–20 min) — between 9 AM and 1 PM, at a consistent daily time**

| Step | Activity | Duration |
|------|----------|----------|
| D1 | EEG baseline: 2 min eyes-closed resting | 2 min |
| D2 | EEG eyes-open: 2 min fixation cross | 2 min |
| D3 | Current emotion rating: SAM valence + arousal + stress (1–9) | 2 min |
| D4 | PANAS-SF (validated 10-item short form): 5 positive affect + 5 negative affect items (Thompson, 2007) | 3 min |
| D5 | Brief context: hours slept, caffeine so far, significant event yes/no | 2 min |

> **Why consistent timing:** Circadian variation in EEG band powers is significant.
> Collecting at a consistent time window controls for time-of-day effects.

---

**EVENING (8–10 min) — within 2 hours of intended sleep time**

| Step | Activity | Duration |
|------|----------|----------|
| E1 | Overall day emotion rating: SAM valence + arousal | 1 min |
| E2 | Peak emotional moment of the day: intensity rating (1–9) + emotional direction (positive/negative) | 1 min |
| E3 | Food/eating section (see Section 2.3) | 4 min |
| E4 | Context covariates: exercise (none/light/moderate/vigorous), alcohol (drinks), medications taken | 2 min |
| E5 | Intention for sleep: stress level right now (1–9), ready for sleep (yes/no) | 1 min |

---

**OVERNIGHT — optional but strongly encouraged**

Participants are invited (not required) to wear the Muse 2 headband during sleep.
Given known comfort and artifact limitations of the Muse 2 during sleep (see
Section 2.4), this is optional. Participants who choose to wear it will have
sleep staging and dream-period detection added to their dataset. Those who do not
will have morning dream reports only, which remain analytically valid for all aims.

---

### 2.2 Onboarding Session (~60 min, remote or in-person)

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 0–10 min | Consent review, questions, e-signature |
| 10–25 min | App walkthrough: morning / daytime / evening flows |
| 25–40 min | Practice EEG session (baseline + D1–D5 cycle, live) |
| 40–50 min | Practice dream journal entry (using a recent dream or imagined content) |
| 50–55 min | Overnight sleep wearing instructions (for those who opt in) |
| 55–60 min | Questions, scheduling, compensation structure explained |

Participants receive a written Quick Reference Card (1 page) summarizing the
daily protocol, troubleshooting steps, and researcher contact information.

### 2.3 Evening Food and Eating Questionnaire

Administered via app each evening. Covers meals from the past 12 hours:

**For each main meal (up to 3):**
- Brief description of what was eaten (free text, 1–2 sentences)
- Pre-meal state: *"Before this meal, were you physically hungry, emotionally
  driven, or eating out of habit?"* (3-choice)
- Post-meal state: fullness (1–9) + satisfaction (1–9)
- Eating style: *"Was this meal mindful, rushed, or emotional?"* (3-choice)

**Day-level eating summary:**
- Any food cravings today? (yes/no → if yes: sweet / salty / fatty / comfort food)
- Overall eating satisfaction today (1–9)
- Did any meal feel like emotional eating? (yes/no/unsure)

### 2.4 Overnight EEG — Design and Limitations

Participants who opt in to overnight EEG wear the Muse 2 headband during sleep.

**What we attempt to measure:**
- Gross sleep staging: wake vs. light sleep vs. deep sleep vs. REM (using the
  NeuralDreamWorkshop sleep staging model, 92.98% accuracy on ISRUC benchmark)
- Dream detection: REM-period identification via theta activity + low EMG pattern
  (97.20% accuracy on training data — see honest limitations below)

**Known limitations of Muse 2 for sleep — acknowledged in this protocol:**

| Limitation | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Headband shifts during sleep | Participants instructed to use a soft sleep mask or headband cover to stabilize; forehead strap secured before sleep |
| Movement artifacts (turning over) | Epochs with amplitude > 75 µV flagged and excluded; only valid epochs analyzed |
| TP9/TP10 contact degrades lying down | TP9/TP10 data treated as supplementary; primary sleep features derived from AF7/AF8 only |
| Comfort: 30%+ of participants may remove device during night | Treated as missing data; participants with < 4 hours of valid overnight recording excluded from sleep-EEG sub-analyses |
| Dream detector accuracy on real home sleep is unknown | Self-reported dream recall (morning journal) is the **primary** dream measure; EEG-detected dream periods are **secondary and exploratory** |

**The overnight EEG sub-study is exploratory.** All four primary aims can be
answered using morning self-report dream ratings alone. Overnight EEG adds
mechanistic depth (REM correlation) but is not required for any primary analysis.

## 3. PARTICIPANTS

### 3.1 Sample Size

**Target enrollment: N = 30 participants**
Assuming 20% attrition over 30 days, effective analytical sample: N ≥ 24.
Assuming average of 25 valid days per participant (some days missed): total
observations ≈ 600–750.

**Power analysis for primary aim (H1 — multilevel model):**
Using MLPowSim and established benchmarks for multilevel longitudinal studies
(Maas & Hox, 2005):
- Assumed within-person effect: β = 0.25 (small-medium; conservative for
  continuity hypothesis)
- ICC (within-person clustering): 0.40
- Level-1 units (days) per person: 25
- Required N for 80% power at α = 0.05 (two-tailed): **N = 22 persons**
- Target N = 30 provides > 90% power and buffer for attrition

**Power analysis for Aim 3 (nightmare prediction — logistic MLM):**
- Estimated nightmare base rate: 15% of nights (conservative community sample estimate)
- At N=30 × 25 nights = 750 nights, expected nightmare observations: ~112
- Adequate for mixed-effects logistic regression with 3–4 predictors

### 3.2 Inclusion Criteria

- Age 18–65 years
- Fluent English speaker (for questionnaire and dream journal comprehension)
- Typically sleeps 6–9 hours per night (self-reported)
- Able to commit to 30 consecutive days of daily data collection
- Has access to a smartphone (iOS or Android) with Bluetooth capability
- No current diagnosis of a neurological condition known to substantially alter
  EEG signal
- Able to provide informed consent

### 3.3 Exclusion Criteria

- Current diagnosis of epilepsy, seizure disorder, or history of seizures
- Current diagnosis of Parkinson's disease, dementia, or active TBI with ongoing
  neurological symptoms (resolved TBI > 2 years with no sequelae: PI discretion)
- Active diagnosis of a psychotic disorder (e.g., schizophrenia)
- Current use of medications with strong EEG effects: benzodiazepines,
  antipsychotics, anticonvulsants, or amphetamine/methylphenidate-based
  stimulants. (SSRIs/SNRIs permitted; recorded as covariate.)
- Current diagnosis of a primary sleep disorder (insomnia disorder, sleep apnea,
  restless legs syndrome, narcolepsy) — these significantly alter sleep architecture
  and dream patterns, confounding the primary aims
- Habitual sleep duration < 5 hours or > 10 hours per night (atypical sleep
  architecture likely to produce non-generalizable results)
- Currently experiencing a major acute psychiatric crisis (active suicidal ideation,
  acute manic episode) — dream content questions may be destabilizing; participant
  welfare takes priority
- Scalp conditions, open wounds, or skin conditions on the forehead or temporal
  region preventing safe headset use
- Pregnancy (hormonal effects on EEG, sleep architecture, and dream content are
  known confounds)
- Participation in another EEG research study in the past 30 days
- Night-shift workers or those with highly irregular sleep schedules (circadian
  misalignment produces atypical EEG and dream patterns)

### 3.4 Recruitment

Participants will be recruited via:
- Flyers posted at local gyms, coffee shops, community centers, and college campuses
- Online postings (Nextdoor, Craigslist, local Facebook groups, Reddit r/[local city])
- Posts in dream journaling and wellness communities (r/DreamJournaling, r/sleep)
- Word-of-mouth referrals from enrolled participants

Enrollment will be first-come, first-served until N = 30 is reached, with a
waitlist maintained in case of early dropout.

## 4. PROCEDURES

### 4.1 Screening

Interested individuals complete a brief online screening survey (Tally or Typeform,
encrypted form platform) covering inclusion/exclusion criteria, with particular
attention to:
- Sleep schedule regularity (required: consistent wake time ± 1 hour)
- Primary sleep disorder history (exclusionary)
- Medication list review (see exclusion criteria)
- 30-day availability and willingness to commit to daily protocol

Eligible candidates are contacted within 3 business days to schedule onboarding.

### 4.2 Informed Consent

Conducted during the onboarding session. The researcher reviews the consent form
verbally, answers all questions, and obtains e-signature (or wet signature for
in-person). Participants receive a copy. Specific consent is obtained for:
- Daily EEG recordings
- Dream journal content (sensitive personal data)
- Optional overnight EEG (separate optional consent checkbox)
- Future de-identified data sharing as an open dataset

### 4.3 Device Provision and Training

**Device requirement (BYOD — Bring Your Own Device):**
This study requires participants to own a Muse 2 EEG headset. Device ownership is
confirmed during screening (Question 15 of the screening survey). Participants who
do not own a Muse 2 are not eligible. No devices are loaned, mailed, or purchased
by the researcher for this study.

**Training during onboarding:**
- Correct headset fit (forehead placement, ear hook positions)
- Signal quality check (SQI ≥ 60% on all channels required to proceed)
- How to run a daytime EEG session via the app
- Dream journal best practices: record immediately on waking, before phone use
- Overnight wearing: position headset, press firmly before sleep, use headband
  cover if needed to stabilize

### 4.4 Daily Protocol Adherence

**Compliance monitoring:**
- App logs timestamp of each completed session
- Push notifications sent at participant's pre-set morning, daytime, and evening
  times as reminders
- Days with < 2 of 3 sessions completed are flagged as "partial days" and
  excluded from primary analyses
- If participant misses 3 consecutive days, researcher contacts them by email to
  check welfare and offer protocol troubleshooting
- Scheduled welfare check-ins: researcher sends a brief email at Day 7 and Day 21
  to all active participants, checking on app function, signal quality, and general
  wellbeing. These are not data collection events — they are welfare monitoring
  and technical support contacts.

**Minimum valid days:**
- Participants completing < 15 valid days (of 30) are excluded from primary
  analyses but retained in the dataset with consent
- Participants are compensated proportionally regardless of completion rate

### 4.5 Dream Journal Protocol

Participants are instructed:
1. Keep the app open on their phone screen before sleeping (on nightstand)
2. On waking, before any other activity, open the app and begin the morning flow
3. Type as much as remembered — even fragments count
4. If no dream is recalled: select "No recall" — this is a valid data point
5. Rate the dream's emotional tone even from partial memories

**No recall data:** Days with "no recall" are retained. Dream recall rate itself
is analyzed as a secondary variable (is low-recall related to stress EEG patterns
from the prior day? Higher stress may impair dream recall via cortisol effects on
hippocampal consolidation).

### 4.6 Offboarding Session (~20 min, remote)

| Activity |
|----------|
| Final EEG session (daytime protocol, standard) |
| Brief semi-structured debrief: participant's own observations about their emotion-dream patterns over 30 days |
| Study explanation: reveal the primary hypotheses being tested |
| Return mailing label sent for device (if on loan) |
| Final compensation issued |
| Invitation to be notified when results are published |

## 5. RISKS AND BENEFITS

### 5.1 Potential Risks

**Physical risks: Minimal.**
The Muse 2 uses passive dry electrodes. No electrical current is delivered. Mild
scalp pressure is the most common discomfort. For overnight wearing, some
participants may find the device uncomfortable during sleep and remove it — this
is expected, permitted, and has no safety consequence.

**Psychological risks: Low to moderate (higher than single-session study).**
Keeping a daily dream journal for 30 days increases engagement with dream content,
which may occasionally include distressing, disturbing, or confusing material.
Some participants may experience:
- Increased salience of negative dream content (attention to nightmares they would
  otherwise dismiss)
- Mild distress on mornings following particularly negative dreams
- Emotional discomfort from tracking their own stress patterns over 30 days

**Mitigations:**
- Participants are reminded at onboarding and in the app that they may skip any
  morning entry if a dream was too distressing to record
- The app includes a daily check-in question: "How are you feeling right now?"
  with a follow-up prompt and mental health resource links if the rating is ≤ 2
  out of 9
- The researcher checks in with each participant by message at Day 7 and Day 21
  to assess welfare and answer questions
- A full list of mental health resources (crisis lines, therapist finders) is
  provided at onboarding

**Privacy risks: Moderate (dream content is sensitive).**
Dream journals may contain personally sensitive material. These are pseudonymized
and encrypted (see Section 7). Dream journal text is stored in the research
database under the participant's study ID only. Researcher reads dream journals
only for data quality checks (e.g., ensuring the entry is dream-related and not
accidentally left blank). Dream content is never shared externally in identifiable
form.

**Dropout and non-completion:** Participants who find the 30-day commitment
burdensome may withdraw. This is permitted and has no penalty. Compensation for
completed days is provided upon withdrawal.

### 5.2 Benefits

**Direct benefits:** No direct medical or therapeutic benefit guaranteed. Many
participants report finding daily emotion tracking and dream journaling personally
meaningful and self-insight-generating. This is an informal finding from similar
longitudinal self-monitoring studies, not a guaranteed outcome.

**Societal benefits:**
- First naturalistic test of the continuity hypothesis with objective physiological
  measures — advances fundamental dream science
- First longitudinal reliability benchmark for consumer EEG emotion monitoring —
  advances wearable neuroscience methodology
- Nightmare prediction proof-of-concept — potential clinical application in
  nightmare disorder, PTSD, and anxiety
- Foundation for the multi-phase Emotional Transfer Chain research program

### 5.3 Risk-Benefit Assessment

Risks are minimal-to-low and manageable with the mitigations described. The
study does not induce any emotional states or administer any substances.
Psychological risk is comparable to that of a well-designed diary study.
The potential scientific contribution is significant. The risk-benefit ratio
is favorable for conducting this research under expedited review.

## 6. COMPENSATION

Participants are compensated **$5 per valid day completed** (valid = ≥ 2 of 3
sessions: morning + daytime + evening), paid as a gift card at the end of the
study or at withdrawal.

| Scenario | Compensation | Volunteer — no payment |
|----------|-------------|
| All 30 days completed | $150 |
| 25 days completed | $125 |
| 15 days completed (minimum for analysis) | $75 |
| Withdrew before Day 15 | $5 × days completed |

**Bonus:** Participants who complete ≥ 28 days AND wear the overnight EEG on
≥ 10 nights receive an additional $25 bonus gift card.

Compensation is not contingent on data quality — only on completion of sessions.

**Non-coerciveness check:** At $5/day, a participant completing all 30 days
earns $150. This is commensurate with 30 days × ~30 min/day = 15 hours total,
at an effective rate of $10/hour — reasonable and non-coercive for a community
sample.

## 7. DATA SECURITY AND PRIVACY

All data security measures described in `04_DATA_SECURITY_PLAN.md` apply in full.
Additional considerations specific to this study:

### 7.1 Dream Journal Content

Dream journal entries are free text and may contain sensitive personal material.
Specific protections:

- Stored under pseudonymized study ID only (no participant name)
- Accessible only to the PI for data quality verification
- Not used for any purpose other than the research aims described in this protocol
- Text is processed by OpenAI's GPT API for emotional valence extraction. API calls
  are made server-side with no participant identifiers transmitted; only the dream
  text itself is sent. Under OpenAI's API terms of service (as of 2026), data
  submitted via the API is not used to train OpenAI's models. This third-party
  processing is disclosed in the informed consent form.
- AI-extracted valence scores are stored; raw dream text is retained for 7 years
  then destroyed.

### 7.2 Longitudinal Data Sensitivity

30 days of daily emotional state, eating behavior, and dream content creates a
detailed behavioral profile of each participant. This is more sensitive than a
single-session dataset. Additional protections:

- The database stores data in daily records keyed only to study ID + date offset
  (Day 1, Day 2... not actual calendar dates) — further reducing re-identification
  risk
- Aggregate-level results (group means, correlations) are the only outputs shared
  publicly; no individual time-series are published

### 7.3 De-identification

As described in 04_DATA_SECURITY_PLAN.md:
- 6-character alphanumeric study ID assigned at enrollment
- Master linkage file stored separately, encrypted, accessible only to PI
- All research data uses study ID only

## 9. STATISTICAL ANALYSIS PLAN

### 9.1 Primary Analysis — Aim 1 (Continuity Hypothesis)

**Model:** Linear mixed-effects model (LME) with participant as random effect.

```
Dream_Affect_Valence(t) ~
    FAA(t-1)           # daytime FAA predicting that night's dream valence
  + HighBeta(t-1)      # daytime high-beta
  + FMT(t-1)           # frontal midline theta
  + SAM_Valence(t-1)   # self-reported daytime valence (tests EEG vs self-report)
  + Day_Number         # linear time trend
  + (1 + FAA | Participant)  # random intercept + slope per person
```

**Outcome:** Morning dream SAM valence rating (1–9)
**Key test:** Fixed effect of FAA(t-1) significantly negative (β < 0, p < 0.05)
**Secondary:** Does EEG (FAA) predict dream valence above and beyond self-report
alone? (Model comparison: full model vs. SAM-only model, likelihood ratio test)

### 9.2 Secondary Analysis — Aim 2 (Eating Behavior Moderator)

**Model:** LME with interaction term.

```
Dream_Affect_Valence(t) ~
    FAA(t-1) * EatingType(t-1)   # interaction: FAA × emotional/physical/habit
  + HighBeta(t-1)
  + SAM_Valence_Evening(t-1)
  + (1 | Participant)
```

**Key test:** FAA × EatingType interaction significant (F-test). Specifically:
the FAA → dream valence relationship is stronger (steeper negative slope) on
emotional eating days than on physical hunger days.

### 9.3 Tertiary Analysis — Aim 3 (Nightmare Prediction)

**Model:** Mixed-effects logistic regression.

```
Nightmare(t) ~ Bernoulli(p)
logit(p) ~
    HighBeta_Quartile(t-1)     # binary: top quartile vs rest
  + FAA_Negative(t-1)          # binary: FAA < 0 vs ≥ 0
  + SAM_Stress_Evening(t-1)    # self-reported evening stress
  + (1 | Participant)
```

**Outcome:** Nightmare flag (yes = 1, no = 0), morning report
**Key test:** AUC of fixed-effect predictions ≥ 0.70 (clinically meaningful
discrimination)

### 9.4 Quaternary Analysis — Aim 4 (Longitudinal Reliability)

- **ICC(2,1)** (two-way random, single rater) for daily FAA across 30 days
- Separate ICCs computed for FAA, high-beta, FMT, and SAM valence
- Target: ICC ≥ 0.50 (moderate reliability) for FAA
- Exploratory: does ICC improve after excluding days with SQI < 60%?

### 9.5 Missing Data

- Days with < 2 of 3 sessions completed: excluded from primary analyses
- Days with SQI < 60% for > 30% of EEG recording: EEG data excluded;
  questionnaire data retained
- "No dream recall" mornings: retained as a valid observation (dream valence
  coded as missing for Aims 1–3; dream recall rate analyzed separately)
- Multiple imputation (m = 20) used for missing covariates (not outcomes)

### 9.6 Multiple Comparisons

Bonferroni correction applied across the three primary hypothesis tests (H1–H3):
adjusted α = 0.017 per test. Aim 4 is descriptive — no correction applied.

## APPENDIX B: NIGHTMARE RISK SAFEGUARD PROTOCOL

If a participant reports a nightmare that caused significant distress, or rates
their morning emotional state ≤ 2 (out of 9), the app displays:

> *"It sounds like you had a difficult night. Would you like to take a break
> from the study today? You are always free to skip any day. Here are some
> resources if you'd like to speak with someone:"*
> [Displays mental health resource list]

The researcher is not automatically notified — participant privacy is preserved
unless they choose to reach out. However, if a participant rates morning state
≤ 2 on three consecutive mornings, the app prompts them to optionally contact the
researcher or a support resource.
